Design envelope

What the report says this bike actually is

The report validates the overall architecture but removes any illusion that this is a casual parts-bin conversion. This version follows that tone.

Architecture

Run the BBSHD-class motor as a bare sensored BLDC core: three phases, Hall sensors, and optional thermistor into the Baserunner V6_Z9. Control comes through the Superharness and SW102 instead of a Cycle Analyst.

Supported controller modeDisplay-driven on/off

Report-default operating posture

  • Start with regen disabled or tightly limited until the drivetrain proves backdrive and the BMS charge limits are deliberate.
  • Treat battery and phase current limits as thermal protection for the controller, connectors, and wiring.
  • Plan an external wheel speed input before trusting speed display or speed limiting behavior.

Controller and controls

Supported topology, but only with the right assumptions

The report does not reject the Baserunner plus Superharness stack. It does reject casual wording around firmware certainty, power accuracy, and speed sensing.

Baserunner mode support

  • The manual supports Cycle Analyst mode, Superharness or third-party display mode, and headless mode.
  • In this build, the SW102 handles on-off and assist UI while the Superharness conditions throttle and brake signals.
  • The page now states plainly that the Z9 is a small-motor-class controller being used in a higher torque mid-drive application.
Mode support keptCurrent-limit aggressively

What must be verified instead of assumed

  • Use the Phaserunner software suite and confirm a Superharness-compatible firmware/software pairing before blaming wiring.
  • Grin documents a known KM5s current and watt scaling mismatch in Superharness setups, so display numbers are not enough for validation.
  • Decide how the auxiliary motor line is being used before final harness termination: thermistor, wheel speed, or a supported shared strategy.
Software state is a gating itemNo hard-coded firmware promise
Speed sensing is the big mid-drive correction

The report is explicit: motor RPM is not wheel speed on this build. If you want accurate speed display or speed limiting, add a wheel magnet pickup and configure it intentionally.

Regen is not presumed useful

Even if the signal path is correct, the BBSHD freewheel may prevent the wheel from backdriving the motor. The page now treats regen as disabled or tightly limited until the hardware proves otherwise.

Frame and fork hard limits

  • 68 mm BB shell and 120 mm rear spacing remain the foundational fit constraints.
  • Stock seatpost is 26.8 mm and the frame listing carries a 6 Nm seat clamp torque note.
  • The Zephyr's 44 mm max spacer stack and 26.4 mm crown race seat are now explicit requirements.
  • The fork axle-to-crown matches the stock geometry expectation, so the main steering change comes from rake, not length.
Published dimensions retainedMeasure before cutting steerer

Cockpit compatibility

  • B263AA family bars use a 25.4 mm stem clamp and a 22.2 mm lever area, which helps throttle and brake control fit.
  • The roughly 17 mm inner diameter at the tips limits bar-end lever and extension options.
  • Brake lever compatibility still needs physical verification; the report does not bless the prior clamp assumption.

Brake reach is not settled by catalog copy

The report found 39-49 mm and 39-51 mm claims for the 9000Ti. The compliant rule is simple: install the wheel and tire, measure bolt-to-brake-track distance front and rear, then cut the fork only after that.

Wheels, tires, and drivetrain reality

The track-thread correction is now explicit

The report singled out rear hub threading as the most important wording fix. This page now treats the lockring interface as a safety-critical detail.

Track hub interface

  • Main cog thread: 1.37 x 24 TPI.
  • Outboard lockring thread: 1.29 x 24 TPI left-hand.
  • A freewheel can share the main thread standard, but that does not erase the lockring function or the need to verify shoulder and spacer clearance.
  • The old same-thread wording is gone because it was too loose for a fixed hub discussion.
Safety-critical correction

Rims, tires, and brakes

  • TB14 numbers now match the report: 14.1 mm depth, 23 mm outer width, ERD 610, about 490 g.
  • If the rim is anything other than TB14, ERD must be verified before spoke decisions.
  • 700x23 Paselas fit, but the report warns that they are still a harsh, limited tire choice for a cargo-capable e-assist configuration.
Loaded ride feel may justify 25-28 mm tires
Two operating modes means two chainline realities

Fixed and freewheel setups on opposite hub sides are not the same chainline problem. The compliant page treats them as separate measurements, not a single assumed offset.

Battery and BMS

Commission the battery side with more caution than the first page had

The report sharpened the BMS guidance in two ways: wake-up behavior is conditional rather than universal, and the small connector telemetry pins deserve explicit hazard treatment.

Battery and BMS behavior

  • 14s4p Li-ion means 58.8 V full charge and about 52 V nominal.
  • The JK BD4A family manual describes charging activation mode. Start around charger voltage = pack voltage + 2 V, then adjust conditionally near full charge without exceeding cell limits.
  • Charge and discharge MOS behavior is configurable, so regen settings and BMS thresholds must be reconciled intentionally.
Wake-up guidance softened to match reportTiny controller headroom remains

Electrical risk notes

  • The VGPS pin on JK auxiliary ports is commonly documented as near pack voltage and sits beside 3.3 V UART pins. Cap or insulate it unless you truly need telemetry.
  • Do not assume throttle rollback equals usable braking force. The report treats regen as off or minimal until proven mechanically and electrically.
  • Use anti-spark or precharge practices as part of normal operation, not as a later refinement.
High-voltage small connector hazardCap unused telemetry ports
Default regen stance

Because the controller max input is 60 V and the pack is 58.8 V at full charge, the report pushes regen from feature to risk item. This page now defaults to regen off or tightly limited until backdrive behavior and BMS charge limits are proven.

Quick triage

Fast report-aligned fault framing

These are not generic e-bike symptoms. They are the failure modes the report makes most plausible on this exact architecture.

SymptomMost likely causeFirst action
Display shows believable numbers but speed is obviously wrong.No external wheel speed input on a mid-drive.Add or configure a wheel speed pickup before using speed data for anything important.
Displayed amps or watts do not match real draw.Known KM5s scaling issue in the Superharness ecosystem.Validate against Phaserunner software or external measurement.
BMS refuses to wake after wiring checks pass.Charging activation condition is not being met.Try a charger condition starting around pack voltage plus 2 V, then adjust carefully within safe cell limits.
BMS trips during downhill braking or with a full pack.Regen voltage or charge current is not reconciled with BMS thresholds.Disable or reduce regen immediately and revisit both controller and BMS limits.
Motor runs rough, cogs, or jerks under light command.Hall wiring, Hall state, or conversion tuning issue.Re-verify Hall behavior and repeat controller setup from a known-good baseline.
Throttle rollback produces no actual braking force.The BBSHD freewheel is decoupling the wheel from the motor.Treat it as expected until proven otherwise; do not confuse signal presence with real regen torque.
Loaded bike feels nervous or twitchy.Reduced trail plus rear-biased weight and small tires.Try wider tires first, then reconsider battery placement before blaming the entire frame.
Freewheel mode and fixed mode want different chain alignment.Opposite hub sides create separate chainline realities.Measure both modes independently and decide where the compromise belongs.
