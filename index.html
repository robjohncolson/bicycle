elec Part 1: BBSHD → Baserunner Z9 Wiring

The BBSHD's stock controller is removed. The motor is treated as a bare 3-phase BLDC with Hall sensors and a thermistor. All 9 wires route through a single Z910 connector on the Baserunner V6 Z9. You build an adapter harness by cutting a Z910 extension cable and splicing to the BBSHD's bare leads.

Throttle-only build — simplified wiring No PAS sensor, no speed sensor, no regen. The BBSHD internal clutch means the wheel cannot backdrive the motor. The 6-pin PAS plug (Z609) and WP8/CA plug (Z812) get capped and ignored.

The Three Categories

Not all 9 wires have the same rules. Understanding which category each wire falls into prevents damage and saves time.

Non-Negotiable
2 Power Wires
Hall 5V and Hall GND. Must be correct. Get these backwards or crossed with a signal wire and you fry the Hall sensors on the motor's sensor board. Red to red, black to black. Verify with DMM before powering anything.
Software Sorts It Out
3 Phase Wires
The thick power wires. Pick any assignment. Worst case: motor vibrates, cogs, or spins backward. No damage. Autotune detects parameters regardless. Wrong direction = flip in software. Cogging = swap any two. At most 2 physical tries.
Methodical Trial
3 Hall Signal Wires
Software does NOT auto-sort these. 6 possible permutations, only 1 is correct for a given phase wiring. Start with the community mapping. If motor runs rough, swap pairs until smooth. Each attempt ~30 seconds.

Plus one more wire: the temperature thermistor (low stakes — wrong mapping just gives no reading or a nonsense value, no damage).

Category 1: Hall Power — Non-Negotiable

These two wires must be correct — no trial and error Reversing 5V/GND or crossing either with a signal wire will destroy the Hall sensors on the BBSHD's internal sensor board. This is irreversible without replacing the sensor PCB.
#Z910 Wire→ BBSHD WireVerification
1 Red (thin) Red — Hall 5V DMM: confirm 5V present when controller powered. No short to GND.
2 Black (thin) Black — Hall GND DMM: no continuity to phase wires or motor case.
Pre-power verification Before connecting anything to the controller, use your DMM to confirm: (1) no short between the two Hall power wires, (2) they match the wires you identified during your bench Hall test (step 5 in the build checklist).

Category 2: Phase Wires — Software Sorts It Out

Three thick wires carrying up to 55A peak. The Baserunner's Autotune will detect motor parameters (kV, Rs, Ls) regardless of which phase goes where.

#Z910 Wire→ BBSHD WireTypeNotes
3 Blue Phase Phase A (thick) HIGH CURRENT Start color-to-color. Anderson Powerpole connectors (45A-rated). Keep runs short, away from signal wires.
4 Green Phase Phase B (thick) HIGH CURRENT
5 Yellow Phase Phase C (thick) HIGH CURRENT

There are only three outcomes that matter:

OutcomeWhat HappensFix
Correct forwardMotor spins smoothly in the right directionDone. Save parameters.
Correct backwardMotor spins smoothly but in the wrong directionClick "Flip Motor Spin Direction" in software. Re-run. Don't swap wires.
Wrong sequence (cogs)Motor vibrates, jerks, or refuses to spinSwap any two of the three phase wires. Re-run Autotune. At most 1 more try.
Phase wire safety Anderson Powerpole crimps must be solid and fully seated. A loose connection under load = arc flash risk. Zip-tie or clamp the harness so vibration happens away from connectors. No phase wire can contact the frame or any signal wire.

Category 3: Hall Signal Wires — Methodical Trial

Three thin wires. The Baserunner's Hall table detection reads whatever sequence the Halls produce, but it trusts the mapping. If the mapping is wrong, the table it builds will be wrong and the motor will run rough, cog, or stutter under load. The software does not try all permutations for you.

6 possible permutations. Only 1 gives correct commutation for a given phase wiring.

Start with the community-reported mapping

#Z910 (thin)→ BBSHD (thin)Type
6 Yellow Hall Grey (Hall A) LOGIC
7 Green Hall Blue (Hall B) LOGIC
8 Blue Hall White (Hall C) LOGIC
If the community mapping doesn't work Run Autotune, run Hall table detection, apply gentle throttle. If the motor spins smoothly — you're done. If it cogs, stutters, or runs rough: swap two of the three Hall wires (any two), re-run Hall table detection, test again. You may need to do this up to 5 more times to cover all 6 permutations. Each attempt takes about 30 seconds.

All 6 permutations (systematic reference)

#Z910 Yellow →Z910 Green →Z910 Blue →Status
1GreyBlueWhiteTry first (community)
2GreyWhiteBlueSwap Green↔Blue from #1
3BlueGreyWhiteSwap Yellow↔Green from #1
4BlueWhiteGreyRotate all from #1
5WhiteGreyBlueRotate other way from #1
6WhiteBlueGreySwap Yellow↔Blue from #1

Work through these in order. When one produces smooth operation, stop and save parameters. Mark which permutation worked on your unit.

Hall/temp wires — solder + heatshrink Low-current logic signals. Solder each splice, stagger the joints so you don't create one thick lump, use adhesive-lined heatshrink on each joint. Then wrap the bundle in an outer sleeve or loom. If you want disconnectability, use a small locking connector (JST, Molex) — but avoid bare spades, they vibrate loose on logic signals.

elec Part 2: JK BMS → 14S4P Battery Pack

The JK-BD4A17S4P is a 4–17S smart BMS with active balancing and Bluetooth. It switches the negative leg only (B+ passes through direct). The balance harness connects via two board connectors: P1 (B- through B8) and P2 (B+ board power, then B9–B17, of which only B9–B14 are used for a 14S config).

52V is dangerous The pack produces 58.8V at full charge and can deliver 40A+ continuous. A short across the balance leads or main terminals can cause arc flash, fire, or severe burns. Work slowly, one wire at a time, and verify every connection with a DMM before plugging into the BMS board.

Balance Wire Connection (P1 + P2)

Connect the balance harness to the pack first (not into the BMS yet). Wire in strict order from negative to positive.

DMM Staircase Verification

Before plugging P1 and P2 into the BMS board, verify every tap. Probe each balance wire against B- (N0). Voltage should rise in ~3.6V steps.

V Staircase Check
If any step shows an unexpected voltage jump STOP. A voltage that jumps by more than one cell (~3.6V) or drops indicates a miswire — you've skipped a group node or connected to the wrong tap. Disconnect, re-check, and re-measure before continuing. Do not plug into the BMS with a known bad tap.
Only after all 14 taps verified Plug P1 and P2 into the BMS board. The staircase check guarantees no two adjacent pins see more than one cell's voltage, which is the BMS's design assumption. A miswired tap can cause the BMS to misread cell voltages, fail to balance, or trigger false protection events.

P Main Power Wiring
58.8V vs 60V headroom 14S at full charge (58.8V) leaves only 1.2V before the Baserunner's absolute 60V max. Even without regen, back-EMF spikes under sudden load changes can produce transient overvoltage. Never charge above 4.2V/cell while connected to the controller.

BMS Activation & Bluetooth Setup

A First Power-On
JK BMS UART/GPS port (P5) — high voltage hazard If you ever connect an ESP32 for telemetry: P5 pin 1 (VGPS) outputs near full pack voltage (~58V) on a tiny 1.25mm JST connector next to 3.3V logic pins. One rotated connector = dead microcontroller or worse. Verify pinout with DMM before connecting.
